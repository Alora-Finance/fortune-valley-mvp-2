Fortune Valley

Why Game-Based Learning?

Only 57% of U.S. adults are financially literate, yet traditional classroom methods — worksheets, lectures, textbook chapters — consistently fail to engage the students who need this knowledge most. The problem isn't the content. It's the delivery.

Intrinsic Motivation

Games create a reason to care. Students don't learn compound interest because it's on the test — they learn it because they need it to beat their rival and win the city.

Opportunity Cost

Every dollar spent on a property is a dollar not invested. The game makes this trade-off visceral: buy now and fall behind, or invest and overtake.

Compound Interest

Students watch their investments grow in real time. They see the difference between investing on Day 1 versus Day 10 — and they feel it in their balance.

Risk vs. Reward

Stocks can spike or crash. Bonds are safe but slow. Students learn to diversify not from a definition, but from losing money they needed.

How It Works

Students run a restaurant in a growing city, competing against an AI rival to acquire properties. The catch: the only way to earn enough money is to make smart financial decisions.

Manage Your Money in Real Time

The HUD shows your balance, your rival's progress, and the ticking clock. Students navigate between their portfolio, available lots, and investment options — making decisions under pressure that mirror real financial trade-offs.

Build Your City, Beat Your Rival

The isometric city is the scoreboard. Every property you buy lights up on the map. But your rival is buying too — and they don't wait. Students learn that timing, patience, and strategy matter more than speed.

Let's Test This With Students

If we're aligned on the value of game-based financial education, the next step is simple: put Fortune Valley in front of real students and measure what they learn.
