01 The Case for Games

Why Game-Based Learning?

Fortune Valley puts students in a situation where financial knowledge is required to progress. Not tested after the fact, but applied in the moment. That's the core thesis: learning happens when the stakes are real, even in a game.

Intrinsic Motivation

Games create a reason to care. Students don't learn compound interest because it's on the test. They learn it because they need it to beat their rival and win the city. The motivation to win drives the motivation to learn.

Opportunity Cost

Students face the trade-off in real time, not on a worksheet. Every dollar spent on a property is a dollar not invested. They discover through play that the decision to buy now versus wait has real consequences.

Compound Interest

Students watch their investments grow and feel the cost of waiting. They see the difference between investing on Day 1 versus Day 10, and they feel it in their balance.

Risk vs. Reward

Students lose money they actually needed, which makes the lesson stick. High-risk stocks can spike or crash while low-risk stocks grow steadily. They learn the trade-off between safety and growth not from a definition, but from experience.

02 Gameplay

How It Works

Students run a restaurant in a growing city, competing against a rival to acquire properties. The catch: the only way to earn enough money is to make smart financial decisions.

Manage Your Money in Real Time

The HUD shows your balance, your rival's progress, and the ticking clock. Students navigate between their portfolio, available lots, and investment options, making decisions under pressure that mirror real financial trade-offs. They discover that timing matters not from a textbook, but by watching their rival pull ahead.

Build Your City, Beat Your Rival

The isometric city is the scoreboard. Every property you buy lights up on the map. But your rival is buying too, and they don't wait. Students learn by doing that patience and strategy matter more than speed.

03 What Students Learn

Learning Targets

Each target is surfaced through a specific game mechanic. Students encounter the concept by playing, then articulate it in their own words afterward.

Target 1

Opportunity Cost

Every dollar can only be used once. Spending $800 on a lot means not investing it. The rival's 30-day countdown makes this choice visible and urgent.

Student Outcome

Identify a specific moment where spending on one thing prevented them from doing something else, and explain whether that trade-off helped or hurt.

Target 2

Risk vs. Reward

The 8 stocks split into two tiers. High-risk stocks (Tech Corp at 15%, ChipMaker at 22% annual) swing much more than low-risk stocks (PowerGrid, RefreshCo, HealthGroup at 5-6% annual).

Student Outcome

Explain why a higher-return investment is also riskier, and describe a game moment where they chose one or the other and what happened.

Target 3

Time Value of Money

The lot purchase popup shows payback period explicitly (e.g., Bistro costs $3,500, earns $12/day, payback = 292 days).

Student Outcome

Look at a lot's payback time and decide whether buying it makes sense given how many days are left, and explain why earlier investment produces more total return than later investment.

Target 4

Compound Growth

Investment prices update every game day along an expected growth curve. Gains accumulate on top of previous gains. The portfolio graph shows this curving upward over time.

Student Outcome

Describe why their investment grew faster toward the end of a holding period than at the start, and connect that to why pulling money out early costs more than just today's gains.

Target 5

Delayed Gratification vs. Immediate Action

The rival creates real deadlines. Waiting to invest longer gets more return, but the rival buys lots on a countdown.

Student Outcome

Reflect on a specific moment where waiting (to stay invested) or acting immediately (to block the rival or buy a lot) was the right call, and explain the reasoning.

Target 6

Diversification as Risk Management

Players can hold multiple stocks simultaneously. The portfolio panel shows a risk level label based on the mix. Students who go all-in on high-risk stocks face bigger swings than those who mix high and low risk.

Student Outcome

Explain why spreading money across both types reduces the chance of a catastrophic loss, using a moment from their game.

Stretch

Return on Investment Reasoning

The lot popup shows cost and income side by side. Students can compare: buying the Bistro ($3,500, +$12/day, 292-day payback) vs. keeping $3,500 invested in a high-risk stock (22% annual = ~$770/year or ~$2.11/day).

Student Outcome

Use those numbers to argue which is the better use of money given their current game state, and recognize that the "better" answer changes depending on how many days remain and what the rival is doing.

04 Classroom Integration

For Teachers

These questions are designed to be used before or after a play session. They help students connect what happened in the game to the financial concepts behind their decisions.

  1. 01
    At any point did you feel torn between buying a property right away and holding back to invest? What did you decide, and what happened next?
  2. 02
    Did you notice your balance growing faster at some points than others? What do you think caused that difference?
  3. 03
    Which investments felt safest? Which felt riskiest? How did you decide when to take the bigger risk?
  4. 04
    When your rival started buying properties faster, did it change how you managed your money? What did you do differently?
  5. 05
    If you played again, would you approach the first few minutes differently? What would you change and why?
